From Score to Cause

A score tells you what. The skill underneath tells you why.

This rubric sits underneath the six scoring dimensions samdesk already tracks. For each dimension, it names the underlying Dunford-style skills that produce it, flags which to coach first when the score is weak, and gives the CRO a structured answer to the only question that matters in a 1:1: what do we actually work on?

The Three Clusters

Ten skills collapse into three moves.

Every Dunford-style skill falls into one of three postures. Coaching is almost always more productive when framed at the cluster level first — you fix the posture before you fine-tune the tactic.

Cluster 01 · Posture
How you occupy the conversation.
"Who's driving?"
Character and conviction skills. Built slowly through market knowledge and reps. Hardest to coach in a role-play, highest leverage in the first ninety seconds of any call.
  • Point-of-View Framing
  • Problem Discipline
  • Conversation Control
Cluster 02 · Diagnosis
What you extract.
"What's actually going on?"
Investigative muscles. The most teachable cluster through frameworks and live reps. Most reps are adequate here; few are great. Feeds everything downstream.
  • Diagnostic Questioning
  • Listening for Signals
  • Alternative Mapping
Cluster 03 · Teaching
What you give back.
"Did the buyer learn something?"
Synthesis under pressure. Requires a deep library of patterns from prior calls. The hardest cluster to develop and the one that most separates great reps from competent ones.
  • Gap Identification
  • Insight Delivery (Reframing)
  • Impact Quantification
  • Future-State Facilitation
01

The six dimensions, diagnosed.

Each of samdesk's existing scoring dimensions is produced by a specific set of underlying skills. When a score is low, the rubric tells you exactly which skill to work on — and which to work on first.

01

Problem Framing

Does the rep enter with a point of view on what matters in this market, and refine the buyer's framing within the first few minutes?

Scoring Behaviors
5/5Exemplary
Opens with a clear POV. Challenges or refines the buyer's framing in the first 2–3 minutes. The problem gets named differently than the buyer would have named it on their own.
3/5Competent
Has a perspective but waits to share it. Accepts the buyer's framing, then pivots later. No early tension in the conversation.
1/5Failing
Opens with "so tell me about your challenges" and adopts whatever the buyer says as the frame. No refinement, no tension, no teaching.

Secondary

Coach First
Point-of-View Framing

You can't deliver insight without a frame to deliver it from. Until the rep has a defensible POV on the market, every downstream fix is cosmetic. Work on building and rehearsing the opening frame before anything else.

02

Discovery Depth

Does the rep uncover the buyer's actual process, hidden friction, and workarounds — not just a surface list of tools?

Scoring Behaviors
5/5Exemplary
Maps the buyer's actual workflow, not just their tool stack. Surfaces friction the buyer has normalized. Catches hesitation and digs deeper.
3/5Competent
Gets a solid picture of current state. Misses a few chances to probe edge cases or workarounds. Accepts some surface answers.
1/5Failing
Surface-level tool list. Accepts first answers. No follow-ups on hesitation or vague claims. Doesn't understand how the work actually gets done.
Skills That Produce This
Primary
Diagnostic Questioning Listening for Signals
Secondary
Conversation Control Gap Identification
Coach First
Diagnostic Questioning

Depth is built on the base extraction muscle. Teach the "process vs tools" distinction and the five layers of any workflow (inputs, actors, decisions, outputs, exceptions) before working on listening or gap identification.

03

Correct Case Study

Does the rep deploy a case study that matches the buyer's industry, scale, and problem shape — not the rep's favorite default?

Scoring Behaviors
5/5Exemplary
Case study mirrors the buyer's situation on the axes that matter. Rep earned the right to use it via discovery. The buyer sees themselves in it.
3/5Competent
Uses an appropriate case but generic in its relevance. Some analogical stretch required to make it fit.
1/5Failing
Defaults to DoorDash regardless of buyer context. Case selected by habit, not fit. Buyer doesn't see themselves in it.
Skills That Produce This
Primary
Problem Discipline Listening for Signals
Secondary
Insight Delivery
Coach First
Problem Discipline

Reps default to a favorite case when they haven't internalized which case fits which pattern. Build a case-study decision tree (industry × problem-shape × scale) so the rep can match in real time instead of reaching for the familiar.

04

Competitive Positioning

Does the rep name the buyer's current alternative, position against it on the axes that matter, and land the category-level distinction?

Scoring Behaviors
5/5Exemplary
Names the alternative explicitly. Positions on the axes that matter for this buyer. Buyer leaves understanding the category-level difference, not just features.
3/5Competent
Acknowledges alternatives when prompted. Differentiates on some axes but not the most decisive ones. Feature comparison more than category framing.
1/5Failing
Avoids naming competitors. Reactive when the buyer mentions one. Pure feature list, no axes, no category frame.
Skills That Produce This
Primary
Alternative Mapping Insight Delivery
Secondary
Point-of-View Framing
Coach First
Alternative Mapping

You can't position against an alternative you haven't extracted. If the rep can't name what the buyer would do without samdesk — status quo, internal tooling, or a specific competitor — the positioning will always default to feature comparison. Extract first, position second.

05

Demo Scenario Anchoring

Does the demo use the buyer's own scenarios, geography, and stakeholders — or is it a generic feature tour?

Scoring Behaviors
5/5Exemplary
Every click is a callback to something the buyer said. Uses their geography, their stakeholders, their actual incident types. The demo feels built for them.
3/5Competent
Uses buyer context for 1–2 marquee moments; the rest runs on a standard path. Locally relevant but not end-to-end personalized.
1/5Failing
Generic demo path. Tours features instead of solving named problems. No callbacks, no local relevance. The demo could run for any prospect unchanged.
Skills That Produce This
Primary
Impact Quantification Future-State Facilitation
Secondary
Diagnostic Questioning
Coach First
Diagnostic Questioning (upstream)

Weak demo anchoring is almost never a demo problem. It's an extraction problem. If discovery didn't surface the buyer's specific scenarios, the rep has nothing to anchor to. Before touching demo structure, check the discovery that preceded it.

06

Concrete Next Steps

Does the call end with a mutually owned next action tied to a specific problem or decision?

Scoring Behaviors
5/5Exemplary
Call ends with a specific action, a named owner on each side, a date, and a reason. Buyer knows what they committed to and why.
3/5Competent
A next step exists but is partially defined. Date set, owners fuzzy. Action disconnected from a specific decision or problem.
1/5Failing
"I'll send over the deck." / "Let's circle back next week." No owner, no reason, no decision tied to the action.
Skills That Produce This
Primary
Conversation Control Future-State Facilitation
Secondary
Problem Discipline
Coach First
Conversation Control

Next steps are the terminal discipline of steering the whole call. If the rep lost the thread mid-call, there's no thread to land on at the end. Coach the habit of naming a decision the call is working toward in the opening minutes — then closing on it explicitly.

Where to invest first.

Three skills carry two primary contributions each: Listening for Signals, Insight Delivery, and Future-State Facilitation. One is Diagnosis, two are Teaching. Posture skills each carry one primary — but they are upstream of everything else, which means coaching them has compounding effects the matrix alone doesn't capture. The practical read: drill Listening and Insight Delivery for quick lift; build Posture for long-term ceiling.

03

Using this in a 1:1.

The rubric isn't a report format. It's a conversation shape. Three moves turn a score into a coaching plan in under ten minutes.

01

Pull the lowest dimension.

Start with the weakest score, not the rep's preferred topic. The goal is highest-leverage coaching, which usually means the dimension the rep least wants to discuss.

02

Name the coach-first skill.

Don't lecture on the whole dimension. Go straight to the upstream skill the rubric points to. "Your Problem Framing score is low because your opening POV isn't set — let's work on that."

03

Assign one rep.

One skill, one call before the next 1:1, one observable behavior change. Coaching that tries to move three skills at once moves none.

The skill underneath tells you what to do.
