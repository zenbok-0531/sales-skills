The Discovery Framework
Framework Rubric →
Best-in-Class Sales Discovery

Don't find the problem. Reframe it.

A structured conversation that shapes how the buyer thinks — not just a list of questions. Use this framework to lead discovery with a point of view, expose the limits of the status quo, and make your solution the obvious next step.

Core Principle
Discovery isn't about finding problems. It's about helping the buyer understand the right problem.
01

The six stages of great discovery.

A sequence — not a checklist. Each stage builds the next. Skipping forward (especially to demo) is the single most common failure mode.

Stages
  • 01Set the Context
  • 02Current Approach
  • 03Surface Friction
  • 04Impact & Consequences
  • 05Alternative Thinking
  • 06Future State
  • 07Bridge to Solution
01

Set the Context

Lead with a point of view. Frame the problem space before the buyer does.
Goal — guide the conversation before the buyer defaults to current thinking

Don't open with "tell me about your challenges." That cedes the frame to the buyer's existing mental model. Instead, anchor the conversation in what you've seen across the market — and invite them to react.

Example phrasing
"Most organizations we work with don't struggle with X anymore. The challenge is usually Y…"
"How are you handling that today?"
02

Understand the Current Approach

Map what they're doing today — tools, process, people.
Goal — identify systems, workflow, and stakeholders

Before you can contrast, you need a clear picture of "normal." Get specific.

  • Systems and tools in use
  • Workflow or process
  • Who is involved
  • What "normal" looks like day-to-day
03

Surface Friction and Gaps

Identify where the current approach breaks down.
Goal — uncover inefficiencies, limitations, and workarounds

Most buyers have normalized the pain. Your job is to make the friction visible again.

  • Manual work
  • Delays and lag time
  • Lack of clarity or visibility
  • Inconsistent outcomes
  • Reliance on multiple systems
  • Workarounds people stopped noticing
04

Explore Impact and Consequences

Translate problems into real-world impact.
Goal — create urgency by connecting friction to stakes

A problem without consequence is a problem without priority. Move from what's broken to what breaking it costs.

  • Time delays
  • Direct and indirect cost
  • Risk and exposure
  • Customer or stakeholder experience
  • Internal pressure and reputation
05

Introduce Alternative Thinking

Gently reframe the problem. Introduce a new lens.
Goal — differentiate your approach before showing your product

This is where discovery becomes insight. You're not pitching yet — you're changing the criteria the buyer will use to evaluate any solution.

  • Highlight the limitations of current methods
  • Introduce a new category or lens
  • Compare approaches — without naming your product
  • Shift the conversation from "features" to "philosophy"
06

Define the Future State

Let the buyer describe what success looks like.
Goal — get them to articulate your value in their own words

When the buyer names the outcome, they sell it to themselves. Your job is to ask, not to tell.

  • What "better" looks like
  • What changes in their workflow
  • What outcomes measurably improve
  • Who else benefits and how
07 · Transition Out of Discovery

Bridge to Solution.

Only now do you introduce your product. The problem is clear, the stakes are agreed, the criteria are set. Your solution should land as the logical conclusion of a conversation the buyer helped build.

Example phrasing
"Based on what you've described, let me show you how we approach that…"
02

Seven Dunford-inspired practices.

The mechanics above only work if the underlying posture is right. These are the habits that separate great discovery from competent Q&A.

01

Lead, don't follow.

Discovery is not passive Q&A. The rep guides the conversation with a point of view.

You set the frame — or the buyer will.
02

Anchor to a specific problem.

Don't chase random pain points. Stay focused on the problem your solution is built for.

Scope discipline is positioning discipline.
03

Surface the current alternative.

You're always competing with the status quo, existing tools, and internal processes. Make those visible.

If you can't name the alternative, you can't beat it.
04

Create insight, not just data.

If the buyer learns nothing new from the call, they leave with no urgency and no reason to prioritize.

Teach something. Every call.
05

Don't rush to demo.

No product until the problem is clear and agreed upon. Demoing too early turns strategy into a feature tour.

The demo lands when the problem is earned.
06

Stay in the problem longer than feels comfortable.

This is where deals are won. Most reps bail to solution-mode two questions too early.

Lean into the silence.
07

Tie everything to outcomes.

Move the language from features to impact. Buyers don't pay for capability — they pay for consequence.

Outcomes are the only currency that matters.
00

Read the room.

All of the above flexes. Senior buyers want the point of view up front; operators may need to walk through current state first. Adapt, don't script.

The framework serves the conversation, not the other way around.
The Cheat Sheet

If you had to compress it — seven moves.

Print this. Put it next to your monitor. Every call should move through these in order, even if the time spent on each varies dramatically.

  • Frame the problem
  • Understand approach
  • Expose gaps
  • Quantify impact
  • Reframe thinking
  • Define success
  • Show solution

Great discovery reframes the problem so your solution becomes the obvious next step.
